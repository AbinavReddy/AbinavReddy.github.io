When the City Locked Down, Crime Didn't Stop — It Shifted

A data-driven look at how San Francisco's crime landscape transformed before, during, and after COVID-19.

DTU 02806 · Social Data Analysis & Visualization · Week 8 Data Story

The Dataset

San Francisco publishes every police-reported incident through its open data portal. We merged two official datasets — one covering 2003–2017 and a newer one from 2018 onward — to create a single file spanning over two decades of crime records. Each row records the date, crime category, police district, and geographic coordinates of an incident.

From the dozens of crime categories in the raw data, we focus on five that are consistently recorded across the entire period and are meaningful to everyday residents: Assault, Burglary, Larceny/Theft, Robbery, and Vehicle Theft. These five account for the bulk of reported "street-level" crime and let us compare like-for-like across years.

One important caveat before we dive in: what appears in this data is what was reported to or recorded by the police. Changes in enforcement strategy, reporting culture, or staffing levels can make crime appear to rise or fall even when actual behavior hasn't changed (Richardson et al., 2019). We'll keep this in mind throughout the story.

Twenty Years at a Glance

The simplest question is also the most revealing: how have these five crime categories evolved year by year? The chart below plots annual incident counts from 2003 to 2024, with a shaded band marking the COVID-19 period (2020–2021).

Two things jump out. First, Larceny/Theft collapsed in 2020 — not because thieves took a break, but because the targets disappeared. With downtown offices empty, tourist hotspots shuttered, and retail stores closed, the opportunities for theft of personal property simply evaporated (PPIC, 2021).

Second, Vehicle Theft tells the opposite story. Cars sat parked on quiet streets for weeks. Reduced foot traffic meant fewer witnesses. The result: a steep climb from roughly 6,000 incidents in 2019 to over 9,000 by 2023 — the highest level in the entire dataset.

The pandemic didn't reduce crime across the board — it redistributed it. Categories tied to foot traffic fell; categories tied to opportunity and empty streets rose.

Where Did Burglaries Move?

The aggregate numbers show that burglary nearly doubled during COVID. But where in the city did this happen? The heatmap below lets you compare burglary concentrations in two periods. The blue layer shows 2017–2019 (pre-COVID); toggle on the red layer to see 2020–2021 (during COVID).

Pre-COVID, burglary was concentrated in commercial districts — places with storefronts, offices, and warehouses. During lockdowns, many of those buildings added security or were simply empty with nothing to steal. At the same time, residential neighborhoods that previously saw relatively few break-ins experienced a noticeable uptick. Media reports from 2020 confirm this shift, with SFPD noting increased daytime residential burglaries in districts like the Sunset and Richmond (SF Standard, 2021).

The District-Level Picture

The trends and maps above tell a city-wide story, but San Francisco's ten police districts are very different neighborhoods. Did the pandemic affect them equally? The interactive chart below compares average yearly incident counts per district across three periods: Pre-COVID (2017–19), COVID (2020–21), and Post-COVID (2022–24). Click on crime types in the legend to isolate individual categories.

Two districts stand out. Southern — home to SoMa, the convention center, and much of SF's tech office space — lost roughly half its Larceny/Theft during COVID, but that number bounced back sharply in 2022–2024 as offices and tourism returned. In contrast, Tenderloin saw assault figures climb through all three periods, suggesting a problem that the pandemic may have worsened but certainly did not create.

It's tempting to compare districts head-to-head, but population, land area, and the mix of residential vs. commercial use differ dramatically. A high count in Southern doesn't mean it's more "dangerous" than Bayview — it means more incidents are reported there, in a district that includes major transit hubs and attracts hundreds of thousands of daily commuters.

What the Data Can — and Can't — Tell Us

The pandemic acted as a natural experiment on urban crime. When daily routines changed overnight — empty offices, deserted tourist spots, cars parked for weeks — the geography and composition of crime shifted with them. Larceny/Theft plummeted where foot traffic disappeared; vehicle theft and burglary surged where opportunity emerged.

But we should be cautious about over-interpreting. Police staffing, enforcement priorities, and reporting rates also changed during COVID. A drop in recorded incidents could reflect fewer crimes or fewer officers on patrol to record them. Similarly, the post-2022 increases in some categories coincide with a return to normal policing levels, not necessarily a "crime wave."

What we can say with confidence is that San Francisco's crime landscape in 2024 looks meaningfully different from 2019. Vehicle theft has not returned to pre-pandemic levels. Larceny/Theft has rebounded but redistributed. And the district-level data shows that blanket city-wide narratives — "crime is up" or "crime is down" — miss the real story, which is always local and always more complicated than a headline.
