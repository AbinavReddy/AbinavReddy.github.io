When the City Emptied, Crime Didn't Vanish: It Moved

How two decades of San Francisco police data reveal a pandemic that didn't reduce crime, but reshuffled who became a target and where.

DTU 02806 · Social Data Analysis & Visualization · Week 8 Data Story

Every time someone in San Francisco calls 911, files a police report, or an officer responds to an incident, a record is created. The San Francisco Police Department publishes these records through the city's open data portal, making over 2.5 million incident reports from 2003 to 2024 available for public analysis [1]. We merged the legacy dataset (2003–2017) with the current reporting system (2018–present) to build a continuous 21-year picture of crime in the city.

From dozens of categories in the raw data, we focus on five that matter most to residents and are consistently recorded across the entire period: Assault (physical attacks), Burglary (unlawful entry into buildings), Larceny/Theft (stealing property without force), Robbery (theft using force or threat), and Vehicle Theft. Together, these categories capture the "street-level" crime that shapes how safe a neighborhood feels.

A critical caveat: this data shows what was reported to police, not all crime that occurred. If a neighborhood distrusts law enforcement, crimes go unreported. If police priorities change, counts can move without underlying behavior changing. As Richardson et al. (2019) show in their work on "dirty data," crime statistics reflect enforcement patterns as well as criminal behavior [2]. We'll return to this tension at the end.

Core claim: COVID-era San Francisco did not experience a uniform "crime up" or "crime down" shift. Risk was redistributed across crime types and neighborhoods.

Methods note (for transparency).
  • Only five consistently coded categories are analyzed (Assault, Burglary, Larceny/Theft, Robbery, Vehicle Theft).
  • Period comparisons use annualized counts (incidents per year in each period) to make 2-year and 3-year windows comparable.
  • District names are standardized before aggregation to avoid duplicate labels caused by mixed capitalization.

Twenty Years at a Glance

Before the pandemic, what did "normal" look like? The chart below tracks annual incident counts from 2003 to 2024 across the financial crisis, the 2010s tech boom, and COVID-19.

The key feature is divergence: Larceny/Theft and Vehicle Theft move in opposite directions from 2020 onward.

Larceny/Theft drops sharply in 2020 because its opportunity structure collapsed. Pickpocketing requires crowded BART stations; shoplifting requires open stores; package theft requires delivery volume. When foot traffic vanished, so did these crimes. The 2022-23 rebound is incomplete, likely reflecting permanent shifts to hybrid and remote work patterns that reduced commuter density [8]. Examining Table 1, the 28.7% decline represents nearly 12,000 fewer annual incidents, a massive shift driven not by deterrence, but by the absence of targets. This is consistent with opportunity theory documented in pandemic-era studies [3][9].

Vehicle theft surges in the opposite direction. Cars that usually circulated sat parked longer on residential streets, and lower foot traffic reduced informal surveillance ("eyes on the street"). This created extended windows of opportunity for thieves. The surge may also have been amplified by the national "Kia Boys" trend [6], where a TikTok-viral exploit made certain Kia and Hyundai models trivially easy to steal using only a USB cable. By 2023, vehicle theft reached levels not seen since the early 2000s. Table 1 shows a 41.5% increase, over 2,000 additional vehicles stolen per year compared to 2019.

Burglary tells a third story. While not as visually dramatic as the larceny drop or vehicle theft spike, burglary increased by 52.6% during COVID (Table 1). Routine activity theory [4] suggests this reflects a shift: commercial buildings emptied out (reducing guardianship), while many residences remained occupied (increasing guardianship there). The net effect depends on where burglaries concentrate. If burglars targeted commercial properties more than residences pre-COVID, the composition shifted. COVID-19 didn't make San Francisco safer. It changed who was vulnerable and where. The total shifted less than the composition.

This divergence raises a natural question: if crime didn't disappear but moved, where exactly did it go?

Following the Burglaries Across the City

Citywide totals show how much changed, but not where. Burglary is a useful case: the COVID-era increase was not spatially uniform. Use the layer control to compare hotspots in 2017–2019 vs 2020–2021.

The pattern aligns with routine activity theory, where crime risk changes as targets and guardianship change over time [4]. In this interpretation, commercial districts lost some target exposure during shutdowns, while residential areas may have become relatively more exposed. This is a theoretically grounded explanation, not direct causal proof.

San Francisco is not a monolith. The ten police districts experienced the shock differently, and recovery has been uneven.

A Tale of Ten Districts

San Francisco's ten police districts are structurally different. The Tenderloin is a dense urban core; Taraval is largely residential; Southern includes convention and office districts; Richmond is mostly low-rise housing. A single citywide number hides those differences. The chart below disaggregates the story.

Southern best illustrates the foot-traffic mechanism. It includes Moscone, Salesforce Tower, and major Market Street corridors. When commuters stopped arriving in 2020, Larceny/Theft dropped sharply. As activity returned, counts rebounded, but not fully, consistent with hybrid work patterns.

Tenderloin shows a different pattern. The neighborhood has long faced concentrated poverty, homelessness, and open-air drug markets. Overdose death data from the SF Department of Public Health confirms the Tenderloin's disproportionate burden in the fentanyl crisis [10]. Assault rises across all three periods, suggesting a persistent local problem that predates COVID. Not every district trend is a pandemic effect.

Avoid ranking districts by raw counts alone. A high count in Southern reflects heavy daytime population and visitor flow, not necessarily resident risk. Districts differ in size, population, and land use.

Implications for Public Safety Policy

If these patterns persist, evidence-based interventions should be:

  • Larceny prevention: Focus on transit hubs, commercial corridors, and areas with recovering foot traffic (Southern, Central). Consider Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design (CPTED) principles for public spaces.
  • Vehicle theft reduction: Implement neighborhood-level interventions in residential areas (Bayview, Ingleside, Taraval). Improved street lighting, community-based surveillance programs, and targeted enforcement of known chop-shop locations.
  • Tenderloin assault: The persistent elevation suggests systemic issues (housing instability, addiction services, mental health crisis response) that require multi-agency coordination beyond traditional policing.
  • District-specific strategies are essential: Southern needs different interventions than Bayview, which needs different approaches than Tenderloin. Uniform citywide policies will misallocate resources.

Critical caveat: These are data-informed hypotheses, not causal proof. The data shows what changed and where; it cannot prove which interventions would be effective. Policy implementation requires controlled evaluation and community input.

So what can we conclude?

What the Data Can and Cannot Tell Us

Across 21 years of SFPD records, the clearest pattern is redistribution, not uniform decline: opportunity shifted from commercial zones toward residential contexts, and risk shifted from pedestrian theft toward parked vehicles.

But we must be honest about what this data cannot prove. Police incident reports measure recorded crime, not actual crime. Reporting behavior, enforcement focus, and data-collection practices can all change over time [2]. Disentangling "more crime" from "more recording of crime" is notoriously difficult.

Consider a practical example: if someone showed you Figure 1 and claimed "San Francisco got safer during COVID," would the data support that? Partially, since some categories did fall. But vehicle theft surged, burglary migrated to new victims, and the drop in larceny likely reflects the absence of targets rather than the absence of motivated offenders. The data supports a nuanced conclusion: the pandemic reshuffled risk, creating winners and losers depending on where you lived and what you owned.

If a policymaker used this analysis to argue for more patrols in residential neighborhoods, the data would offer suggestive support but not proof. We've shown that burglaries shifted to the Sunset and Richmond; we haven't shown that more police presence would have prevented them. Correlation is not causation, and historical data is not a controlled experiment.

What we can say is this: San Francisco's 2024 crime landscape differs meaningfully from 2019. Vehicle theft is still elevated, larceny has partly rebounded with a different spatial pattern, and Tenderloin assault remains high. "Crime up" vs "crime down" is too coarse; category and place matter.
