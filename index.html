The Pandemic Reshuffle

How COVID-19 transformed where and when crime happens in San Francisco

A data story based on two decades of SFPD incident reports (2003-2024)

About This Data

This story draws on over 2.5 million incident reports filed by the San Francisco Police Department between 2003 and 2024. The dataset is publicly available through DataSF, the city's open data portal, and captures every crime reported to police — from shoplifting to assault, vehicle theft to burglary.

Each record includes a timestamp, crime category, police district, geographic coordinates, and resolution status. We focus on five high-volume crime types: Assault, Burglary, Larceny/Theft, Robbery, and Vehicle Theft. Together, these represent a substantial portion of reported incidents and allow us to trace how the city's crime landscape shifted before, during, and after the COVID-19 pandemic.

A word of caution: Police data reflects reported crime, not all crime. Reporting rates vary by neighborhood, crime type, and community trust in law enforcement. A drop in reported incidents may mean fewer crimes occurred — or it may mean fewer people called police. Throughout this story, we distinguish between what the data shows and what it can prove.

1. Twenty Years at a Glance

San Francisco's crime patterns were surprisingly stable for most of the 2010s. Larceny/Theft dominated the statistics, hovering around 50,000-60,000 incidents per year, while violent crimes like assault and robbery remained relatively flat. Then came 2020.

The pandemic didn't reduce crime across the board — it reshuffled the deck. As shelter-in-place orders emptied downtown offices and closed retail stores, larceny plummeted. Fewer people on the streets meant fewer pockets to pick and fewer storefronts to shoplift. But vehicle theft surged: with more cars parked unattended on residential streets, thieves found easier targets.

The divergence is striking. While overall crime volume fell in 2020, this masked a fundamental change in what kind of crime was happening. Opportunistic property crimes tied to foot traffic declined; crimes targeting parked vehicles and unoccupied buildings increased. The pandemic didn't make San Francisco safer — it changed where vulnerability concentrated.

2. Burglary Moves to the Neighborhoods

Perhaps no crime type illustrates the pandemic's geographic impact better than burglary. Before COVID-19, burglary hotspots clustered around San Francisco's commercial corridors: the South of Market (SoMa) district, the Tenderloin, and the Financial District. These areas had dense concentrations of businesses — and businesses are prime burglary targets.

But when storefronts shuttered and office buildings emptied, burglars adapted. The map below shows how burglary incidents migrated from commercial centers to residential neighborhoods. This geographic redistribution aligns with what criminologists call routine activity theory: crime occurs when motivated offenders encounter suitable targets in the absence of capable guardians. The pandemic didn't change the number of motivated offenders in San Francisco — it changed where targets were available. Closed businesses meant fewer commercial targets; work-from-home policies meant homes were better guarded during the day. So burglaries shifted toward evening hours in residential areas where homes sat empty.

This matters for policy. A policing strategy optimized for pre-pandemic burglary patterns — focused on commercial districts during business hours — would have been poorly targeted in 2020-2021. The data suggests crime prevention needs to adapt as quickly as criminals do.

3. Winners, Losers, and the Recovery

Not all neighborhoods experienced the pandemic equally. Some districts saw dramatic drops in reported crime; others saw increases. The interactive chart below breaks down average yearly incidents by police district across three periods: Pre-COVID (2017-2019), COVID (2020-2021), and Post-COVID (2022-2024).

The Southern and Central districts — covering downtown, SoMa, and North Beach — experienced the largest drops. These areas depend on commuters, tourists, and shoppers. When those populations vanished, so did the crimes they attract. By contrast, the Ingleside and Taraval districts, residential neighborhoods in the city's south and west, saw more modest changes — and in some categories, increases.

The post-pandemic period (2022-2024) shows partial recovery. Downtown crime is rebounding as workers return, but it hasn't reached pre-pandemic levels. Vehicle theft, meanwhile, remains elevated almost everywhere. This suggests that some pandemic-era behavioral changes — like increased car ownership and remote work — may be structural rather than temporary.

What the Data Can't Tell Us

This analysis reveals patterns, but patterns aren't explanations. When we see that drug-related arrests dropped during the pandemic, we cannot conclude that drug use declined. It's equally plausible that enforcement priorities shifted, that officers were reassigned to pandemic response, or that citizens reported fewer incidents when streets were empty.

Similarly, the geographic shift in burglaries doesn't prove that "burglars moved to residential areas" as a conscious strategy. It may reflect changes in reporting behavior (homeowners stuck at home noticed more attempted break-ins), changes in enforcement (commercial districts received fewer patrols), or changes in what got classified as burglary versus other crimes.

The limits of incident data: Police data captures law enforcement activity as much as criminal activity. As Richardson et al. (2019) argue in their analysis of "dirty data" in predictive policing, crime statistics are shaped by department policies, officer discretion, and community reporting patterns — not just by how much crime actually occurs.

If someone used this data story to argue that "San Francisco got safer during the pandemic," they would be overreaching. What we can say is that reported crime shifted in ways consistent with major changes in how people lived and worked. The responsible interpretation is that COVID-19 disrupted crime patterns, not that it reduced crime in any fundamental sense.
